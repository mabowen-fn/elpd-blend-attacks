# 05 — Reading the Plots

## The ELPD Surface Smoke Test Plot

File: `results/plots/elpd_surface_smoke_test.png`

This plot is generated by Cell 5 of `notebooks/phase1_scaffold_and_validation.ipynb`. It is the primary validation that the ELPD blender is working correctly before we ever touch a real model.

---

### What the X and Y Axes Mean

**X-axis: η (blending weight), range [0, 1]**
- η = 0 (left edge): pure query attack — the blended gradient is entirely the NES estimate from the target. The surrogate contributes nothing.
- η = 1 (right edge): pure transfer attack — the blended gradient is entirely the surrogate. The target is not queried for gradient direction.
- η = 0.5 (middle): equal blend of both sources.

**Y-axis: ELPD score (higher = better)**
- This is the LOO cosine ELPD: `mean_i[score_i(η)] - T·std_i[score_i(η)]`
- At any given η, it measures "how well does the blended gradient μ(η) predict the held-out NES draws?"
- Higher score = this η produces a gradient direction that better explains the NES draws from the target model.

---

### Left Plot — Aligned Surrogate

**Setup:** The surrogate gradient `g_sur ≈ g_true + tiny noise` and the target NES draws `g_i ≈ g_true + small noise`. Cosine similarity between surrogate and target is ~+0.999.

**What you see:** The ELPD curve is nearly flat with a slight peak around η = 0.5–0.6. The dashed vertical line marks η* at that peak.

**What this means:**
- The surrogate is so well-aligned that almost any blend is approximately as good as pure query (η=0).
- The slight peak to the right of 0 reflects the surrogate providing a useful noise-reduction effect on the NES estimate.
- In a real attack, this corresponds to the early steps where the target and surrogate agree closely on gradient direction.
- The ELPD score at η=0 and η=1 is nearly equal (~0.9883), which is correct: a cosine of 0.999 means almost all directions are well-predicted.

**Why the peak isn't at η=1:** Even a perfectly aligned surrogate shouldn't get full weight. The NES draws have noise, and weighting them at (1-η) also helps reduce that noise. An intermediate η is genuinely optimal.

---

### Right Plot — Opposing Surrogate

**Setup:** The surrogate gradient `g_sur ≈ -g_true + tiny noise` — pointing in the exact opposite direction from the true gradient. Cosine similarity is ~-0.999.

**What you see:** The ELPD curve drops sharply from η=0 toward deeply negative values as η increases. The dashed vertical line marks η*=0.0.

**What this means:**
- At η=0 (pure query): the blended gradient is just the NES mean. Score ≈ 0.988 (the cosine similarity of the draws with themselves via LOO, which is high because they all point roughly the same direction).
- At η=0.5: mixing in the opposing surrogate sends the prediction halfway to the wrong direction. Score ≈ -0.24.
- At η=1 (pure transfer): the gradient is entirely the surrogate, which points exactly backward. Score ≈ -0.99 (negative cosine = opposing direction).
- The ELPD correctly identifies that η*=0 is optimal — ignore the adversarial surrogate entirely.

**The sharp drop is evidence the system works.** A flat curve here would mean the blender can't detect a surrogate pointing in exactly the wrong direction. The fact that the score falls from +0.99 to -0.99 as η goes from 0 to 1 proves the ELPD is measuring gradient alignment correctly.

---

### What a Failing Plot Looks Like

During debugging we produced several failing configurations. Here's what each looked like and what it meant:

**Flat surface (Mode A failure):**
```
η=0: 0.000  η=0.5: 0.000  η=1: 0.000
```
Completely horizontal line at zero. This was the Gaussian log-likelihood with fixed σ, where the log_const term dominated and was equal for all η. The blender was blind to alignment.

**Monotonically decreasing (Mode B failure):**
```
η=0: -1.564  η=0.5: -1.573  η=1: -1.679
```
A gently sloping line that always peaks at η=0. This was the negative MSE formulation, which correctly detected that η=0 minimises MSE (because g_mean is the centroid of the draws) but therefore always preferred pure query regardless of surrogate quality.

**Zero at origin, negative everywhere (Mode C failure):**
```
η=0: 0.000  η=0.5: -0.106  η=1: -0.287
```
Zero at η=0, increasingly negative for larger η. This was the improvement formula, where the mean of the residuals is exactly zero by the definition of the mean, making every η look like it hurts.

---

### How to Use These Plots During Research

1. **Before any real model runs:** Generate the smoke test plot with synthetic gradients. If both curves have the right shape (left peaks near 0.5–1, right peaks at 0), the blender is working.

2. **During a real attack on one image:** Log `result.elpd_values` and `result.eta_grid` at each step. Plot the ELPD surface at step 1, step 10, step 50. You should see the peak shift leftward (η* decreasing) as the perturbation grows and the surrogate becomes less reliable.

3. **Aggregate analysis:** After running on 100+ images, plot the mean η trajectory (Cell 8 of `experiment.ipynb`). A research finding: if η starts near 0.7 and decays to 0.3 over steps, that's quantitative evidence that surrogate-target alignment degrades as the perturbation grows — a publishable finding that validates the adaptive approach.

## The Query vs ASR Plot (experiment.ipynb Cell 7)

File: `results/plots/query_vs_asr.png`

**X-axis:** Query budget (number of target model queries).  
**Y-axis:** Attack Success Rate — fraction of images successfully fooled within that budget.

**What to look for:**
- The ELPD-Blend curve (blue, thick) should rise steeply early and plateau at high ASR.
- MI-FGSM and DI-FGSM (green dashed) are horizontal lines — they use 0 queries and have a fixed ASR determined by cross-architecture transfer rate.
- NES-only (orange) rises gradually — it needs many queries to get a reliable gradient estimate.
- Static hybrid (purple dotted) should be between NES-only and ELPD-Blend.
- Square attack (red dashed) is score-based and usually competitive at high query budgets.

**The key claim:** The ELPD-Blend curve crosses 90% ASR at a lower query budget than all other query-based methods. This is the core experimental result of the paper.

## The η Trajectory Plot (experiment.ipynb Cell 8)

File: `results/plots/eta_trajectory.png`

**Left panel:** Mean η per attack step, with ±1 std band.  
**Right panel:** Mean cosine similarity between surrogate and target gradients per step.

**What to look for:**
- η and cosine similarity should be positively correlated (when the surrogate aligns well, η should be high).
- Both should generally trend downward over steps — as the perturbation grows, the surrogate becomes less reliable.
- The correlation between the two curves is evidence that the ELPD blender is correctly detecting and responding to changes in alignment.

If η is stuck at 0 or 1 for all steps, something is wrong with the blender (possibly the ELPD surface is still flat for real gradient data). If η and cosine similarity are uncorrelated, the ELPD metric is not measuring what we think it is.
